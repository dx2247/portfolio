01 — Capstone

NeighborHub — hyperlocal mutual aid for NYC

Service Design
Team Project

A platform that connects NYC neighbors to request and offer help — before, during, and after disruptions.

NeighborHub is a verified, hyperlocal service that helps residents build everyday trust before disasters and coordinate urgent support during them. When the subway floods, the power cuts out, or the corner store sells out of milk, neighbors are willing to help — but there's no scalable, trusted way to find each other. We designed one.

NeighborHub
Help nearby · trust local
PROBLEM · 01

Structural Vulnerability

Small apartments, frequent grocery trips, total reliance on transit and delivery.

PROBLEM · 02

Information Gap

No real-time view of what's still available or when supply will return.

PROBLEM · 03

Stockpiling Behavior

Uncertainty triggers panic-buying that empties shelves before scarcity is real.

PROBLEM · 04

Disproportionate Impact

People without strong networks or mobility absorb the cost.

Research · Primary

We interviewed NYC residents across age and life stage — from recent grads in Hell's Kitchen to a 79-year-old in her own building — about how they navigate food access during snowstorms, subway disruptions, and short power outages. A consistent theme emerged: "It's not that I think there will be no food. I just don't know how hard it will be to get it."

Research · Secondary

We reviewed reporting and academic work on Hurricane Sandy, the 2003 and 2019 NYC blackouts, and the 2025 SNAP interruption. The pattern was clear: shortages during urban disruptions are rarely caused by lack of supply. They emerge from breakdowns in coordination — between transit, retail, delivery labor, and information flow.

Problem Statement · Group

New York's infrastructure is designed to run efficiently in normal conditions, but offers little built-in support for residents when systems temporarily fail. During the first hours of disruption, there is no coordinated, resident-centered mechanism to stabilize essential access — leaving individuals to improvise their own recovery.

Three concepts → one direction

We sketched three solutions and tested them with four NYC residents. Each was rated on a skeptical-to-confident scale and discussed openly. The pivot came not from the highest score, but from listening to why people responded the way they did.

Concept Avg. confidence What people said
Real-time food map 6.4 Wanted exact stock counts; worried about wifi during disasters.
Apartment pantry 4.3 Concerns about freshness, food preferences, building conditions.
Emergency pickup network 5.8 Felt similar to existing food banks; questions about who runs it.
Status quo (delivery apps) 3.4 Too expensive, unreliable, unavailable when most needed.

Across all three concepts, one thread kept surfacing in interviews: people wanted a person, not a system. Specifically, they wanted to feel like someone nearby had their back. So we pivoted away from the inventory map and toward the relationship — and NeighborHub emerged.

The product, at a glance

F · 01
Verified Neighborhood Network
Residents join through address verification — a utility bill or lease confirms they actually live in the area. Users are auto-grouped into a 4-block radius neighborhood, large enough to be useful, small enough to feel real.
F · 02
Everyday Mutual Aid
Before any disaster, low-stakes exchanges build familiarity: borrowing a ladder, sharing leftovers, lending a charger, checking in on a neighbor. The network gets warm before it has to work hard.
F · 03
Crisis Coordination Mode
During disruptions, urgent posts are surfaced through push notifications, tagged with location and need, and routed by proximity rather than by feed chronology. No scrolling — direct connections.
F · 04
Trust & Privacy
Reviews, ratings, in-app messaging only, exact addresses hidden. The system is funded as public infrastructure rather than ad-supported — data never becomes the product.

My Role

As one of four team members, I led primary research on the infrastructure-dependence angle — interviewing a young finance professional in Midtown about how she navigated food access during winter storms, and translating that into our individual problem statement. I contributed to ideation, the user-journey mapping, the comparison framework, and the prototype's pre-disaster trust-building flow.

The rest of our team (Ayano, Maria, and Stella) brought additional interview data, the prototype mockups, and the visual identity. The pivot from "food map" to "neighbor network" was a team conclusion drawn directly from user testing.

What I Learned

  • The highest-rated concept isn't always the right one. The why behind a score matters more than the score itself.
  • Emotional resilience and logistical resilience are not separate problems. People asked for inventory data; what they actually wanted was reassurance.
  • Design for the calm. Trust-building happens before the crisis, not during it — anything else is too late.
  • Verification is a design material. The same friction that protects users also signals seriousness, and that signal is part of the value.
Closing

Even minor disruptions can escalate into widespread inefficiency and inequality. The issue is not resource scarcity — it is the absence of coordination and trust at the local level. NeighborHub is an attempt to design that coordination into the city before it's needed.

02 — Co-design

The Effortless Elegant Winter Coat

Co-design Exercise
Individual

A winter coat designed for and with a master's student balancing academic, social, and professional life in NYC.

The brief was small in scope but rich in method: design a single coat for one specific person, then iterate it through a structured co-design conversation — first an original sketch, then an AI-generated variation, then a final design produced together with the client. The point wasn't the coat. The point was watching a person's preferences sharpen against two alternatives.

Co-design Sketching Client Interview Iteration
Client Brief
"I want one coat that works everywhere — class, dinner, an office visit — and I want to grab it and go without thinking."
Need Extreme warmth, lightweight, large pockets. Want One versatile, light-colored coat. Easy mornings. Aspire Mature, polished, calm. Warm without bulk.

Three rounds, one direction

Round 01 · Original

Hand sketch

Mid-length, structured wool-blend shell with a built-in scarf collar, a defined waist seam, and four functional pockets inspired by the Chanel field-jacket silhouette.

  • Built-in scarf collar (no lost scarves)
  • Hidden insulated lining
  • Sand color, structured but soft
  • Mid-length silhouette
Round 02 · AI Variation

Generated alternative

Same brief, run through an image-generation model to produce a more utility- forward interpretation — hood, drawcord waist, sleeve cargo pockets, buckled cuffs.

  • Structured hood (added)
  • Adjustable drawcord waist
  • Sleeve cargo pockets
  • Buckled cuff straps
✓
Round 03 · Co-design

Final, together

Side-by-side review with the client surfaced a clear preference: keep the minimal base, borrow only the structural details that earned their place.

  • Open lapel + integrated scarf
  • Structured belted waist (replaces drawcord)
  • Cuff strap detail (kept)
  • Hood & sleeve pockets removed
What surprised me

The client wasn't choosing between two coats — she was choosing between two moods of mornings. The AI version felt like getting ready for a hike. The original felt like getting ready for a meeting. The final coat is the one she'd actually grab without thinking — which was the original brief all along.

03 — Object Design

The Lift-Top Coffee Table

Furniture Design Exercise
Individual

Designing a single piece of furniture that supports working, eating, and hosting in a small NYC apartment.

The exercise: imagine the constraints of living alone in a small Manhattan apartment — limited square footage, flexible work habits, occasional guests — and design a single piece of furniture that does more than one thing without looking like it's doing more than one thing.

The lift-top mechanism transforms the surface from coffee-table height to dining/working height. Two side compartments offer hidden storage to reduce visual clutter. The rounded oval edges and warm wood-fluted sides make the piece feel safe, calm, and home-like — the goal was to make a small apartment feel less like a cell and more like a place.

Furniture Multi-functional Small-space living Sketching
Why this
01 · Lift-top surface

Combines work, storage, dining, aesthetics. Saves space; supports flexible work habits; makes the user feel supported rather than cornered.

02 · Hidden storage

Keeps items out of sight. The visual quiet helps the room feel calmer and more ordered — the apartment becomes restful instead of cluttered.

03 · Generous size

Large enough to invite friends over. Living alone in NYC, that hospitality is what turns a unit into a home.

The thread across all three projects

A coat, a table, a neighborhood platform. On the surface they have nothing in common. Underneath, they're all asking the same question: how do you design something that lets a person feel a little more at home in the city?
